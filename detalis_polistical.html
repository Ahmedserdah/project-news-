Putin's Victory Day speech

Putin in his Victory Day speech that NATO represents a "clear threat" to Moscow.


Russian President Vladimir Putin blamed the West for the war in Ukraine, during his Victory Day address.
He said that the past year witnessed tension with European countries, the North Atlantic Treaty Organization "NATO",
adding that Russia "is urging Europe for a just settlement, but they do not want to listen" to our demands.

In his speech, the Russian president said that Russian troops and volunteers in the Donbass region of eastern
Ukraine were "fighting for the motherland and its future", and accused the West of preparing to "invade our land".

He added that his "special military operation" was necessary and was the "right decision."
Putin vowed that the state would do "everything" to take care of families grieving the bereavement caused by the Ukraine war.
"The death of any soldier or officer is painful for us," he said.
The Russian president referred to the tension with European countries and NATO,
saying that they were preparing for a punitive operation in Donbass in eastern Ukraine.
“In Kyiv they were saying that they might get nuclear weapons, and NATO began to explore the lands close to us,
and that became a clear threat to our country and our borders. Everything indicated that there was a need to fight,” he said.

The Russian president began his Victory Day speech on Moscow's Red Square by telling Russian
fighters that they are fighting for Russia's security.
He congratulated the veterans on "Great Victory Day", which celebrates the Soviet Union's
victory over Nazi Germany in World War II in 1945.
Putin called for a minute of silence in memory of the Russians killed in World War II,
and the soldiers fighting for Russia in the Donbass.
He ended his speech amid the cheers of soldiers in Red Square, artillery fire and the Russian national anthem.
The celebration featured thousands of soldiers and an air show - including the plane
that Putin would use in the event of a nuclear conflict.
