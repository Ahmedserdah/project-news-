Canaanite goddess statue

A farmer in Gaza finds a 4,500-year-old statue of a Canaanite goddess


A farmer found a stone statue in the Gaza Strip of the Canaanite goddess of beauty, love and war.
Palestinian archaeologists say that the head of the Canaanite goddess "Anat" dates back 4,500 years, in the late Bronze Age.
The farmer, Nidal Abu Eid, found the artifact during excavations on his land, in Khan Yunis, in the southern Gaza Strip.
And via social media, some Gazans made sarcastic comments suggesting that the goddess' association with war seemed appropriate.
In the past few years, they have witnessed a series of devastating explosions in the conflict between Israel and armed groups in the Gaza Strip,
which is run by the Hamas movement.

The discovery of the limestone statue reminds that the sector - part of an important trade route for successive ancient civilizations - was originally a Canaanite settlement.
The 22 cm high sculpture shows the face of the goddess wearing a crown.
Farmer Nidal Abu Eid said, "We found it by chance. It was covered in mud, and we washed it with water."
He told the BBC: "We knew it was a valuable thing, but we didn't know it had great archaeological value."
And he added: "We thank God and we are proud of Him, because he has remained in our land, in Palestine, since the Canaanite era."
The statue of "Anat" - one of the most famous Canaanite deities - is displayed in the Pasha Palace, a historical building that is used as one of Gaza's few museums.
